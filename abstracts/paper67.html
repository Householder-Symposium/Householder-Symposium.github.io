On computing simultaneous Gaussian quadrature rules for Multiple Orthogonal Polynomials

Nicola Mastronardi, Teresa Laudadio, Walter Van Assche, Paul Van Dooren

Abstract

Simultaneous Gaussian quadrature rules associated with multiple orthogonal polynomials (MOPs) were first introduced in [1] to model computer graphics illumination, where the computation of different weighted integrals with the same integrand function was needed. The aim was to minimize the evaluations of the integrand function and maximize the order of the quadrature rules based on the same set of nodes.

MOPs are a generalization of orthogonal polynomials. They originally appeared in Hermite-Padé approximation (simultaneous rational approximation) and number theory. Recently, they turned out to be very useful in random matrix theory, combinatorics and Markov chains.

MOPs can be divided into two classes: type I and type II [2]. Here, we focus on MOPs of type II.

Given \(r \) weight functions \(w^{(i)}(x) \ge 0,\) with support \(\Delta ^{(i)}, \; i=1,\ldots ,r,\) on the real line, the sequence of MOPs \(\left \{p_n(x)\right \}_{ n=0}^{\infty } \) of type II satisfies the following orthogonality conditions [2]:

\(\seteqnumber{0}{}{0}\)

\begin{equation} \label {eq:exactp} \int _{\Delta ^{(i)}} p_{n}(x) x^k w^{(i)}(x)dx = 0, \quad 0\le k \le n_{i}-1, \end{equation}

with \(n= \sum _{i=1}^r n_i. \)

Let \(\Delta = \bigcup _{i=1}^{r}\Delta ^{(i)}. \) Two different systems of MOPs of type II can be considered [7]:

  • 1. Angelesco system, where the open intervals \(\Delta ^{(i)}\), \(\; i=1,\ldots ,r,\) are disjoint, i.e., \(\Delta ^{(i)}\bigcap \Delta ^{(j)}= \emptyset \), for \(i \ne j,\) and the closed intervals \(\Delta ^{(i)}\) are allowed to touch.

  • 2. algebraic Chebyshev system (AT system), where \(\Delta ^{(i)} =\Delta , \; i=1, \ldots ,r. \)

It is known that \(p_n(x)\) of type II has exactly \(n \) zeros in \(\Delta \) [7, Th. 2].

A set of MOPs satisfies an \((r+2)\)–term recurrence relation. Without loss of generality, we focus on the case \(r=2.\) Therefore, the set of monic MOPs satisfies a \(4\)–term recurrence relation

\(\seteqnumber{0}{}{1}\)

\begin{equation} \label {eq:MOP} xp_{i}(x) = a_i p_{i+1}(x) + b_i p_{i}(x) + c_i p_{i-1}(x) + d_i p_{i-2}(x), \quad a_i=1,\;\; i=0, \ldots , n-1, \end{equation}

with \(p_{-2}(x)=p_{-1}(x)=0.\) Writing (2) in a matrix form, we obtain

\(\seteqnumber{0}{}{2}\)

\begin{equation} \label {eq:MOPzeros} H_n \left [\begin{array}{c} p_0(x) \\ p_1 (x) \\ \vdots \\ p_{n-1}(x) \end {array}\right ] + a_{n-1} \left [\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \\p_n (x) \end {array}\right ] = x \left [\begin{array}{c} p_0(x) \\ p_1 (x) \\ \vdots \\ p_{n-1}(x) \end {array}\right ], \end{equation}

where \(H_n \) is the \(n\times n\) banded lower Hessenberg matrix with 2 sub–diagonals and one upper-diagonal :

\(\seteqnumber{0}{}{3}\)

\begin{equation} \label {H} H_n := \left [\begin{array} {ccccccc} b_0 & a_0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \ldots & 0 \\ c_1 & b_1 & a_1 & 0 & 0 & \ldots & 0 \\ d_2 & c_2 & b_2 & a_2 & 0 & \ldots & 0 \\ 0 & d_3 & c_3 & b_3 & a_3 & \ddots & 0 \\ \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & 0 \\ 0 & \ldots & 0 & d_{n-2} & c_{n-2} & b_{n-2} & a_{n-2} \\ 0 & \ldots & 0 & 0 & d_{n-1} & c_{n-1} & b_{n-1} \\ \end {array} \right ]. \end{equation}

By (3), it follows that \(\lambda \) is a zero of \(p_n(x)\) iff \(\lambda \) is an eigenvalue of \(H_n. \)

Simultaneous Gaussian quadrature rules associated with MOPs [2],

\[ \sum _{k=1}^{n}\omega _{k}^{(j)} f(x_j) =\int _{\Delta ^{(i)}} f(x) w^{(j)}(x)dx +E_{n}^{(j)}(f), \quad j=1,2, \]

with \(E_{n}^{(j)}(f)=0\), if \(f \) is a polynomial of degree \(n+n_j-1, \) can be retrieved from the eigenvalue decomposition of the matrix \(H_n \) [2, Th. 3.2], [6]. In fact, the following Theorem holds.
Theorem The nodes \(x_{j}, \; j=1,\ldots ,n, \) of the simultaneous Gaussian quadrature rule are given by the eigenvalues of the banded Hessenberg matrix \(H_n\) (4). Moreover, let us denote by \(\mathbf {u}^{(j)}=[u_1^{(j)}, u_2^{(j)},\ldots , u_n^{(j)} ]^T, \) and \(\mathbf {v}^{(j)}=[v_1^{(j)}, v_2^{(j)},\ldots , v_n^{(j)} ]^T \) the left and right eigenvectors of \(H_n \) associated with \(x_{j}, \) respectively. Then,

\(\seteqnumber{0}{}{4}\)

\begin{equation} \label {eq:weights} {\displaystyle \omega _j^{(1)}}=\frac {\displaystyle v_1^{(j)} f_{1,1}u_1^{(j)}}{\displaystyle \mathbf {u}^{(j)^T} \mathbf {v}^{(j)}},\quad {\displaystyle \omega _j^{(2)}}=\frac {\displaystyle v_1^{(j)}\left ( f_{2,1}u_1^{(j)}+f_{2,2}u_2^{(j)}\right )}{\displaystyle \mathbf {u}^{(j)^T} \mathbf {v}^{(j)}}, \quad j=1,\ldots ,n, \end{equation}

where

\(\seteqnumber{0}{}{5}\)

\begin{equation} \label {eq:D} \begin{array}{ll} f_{1,1}={\displaystyle \int _{\Delta ^{(1)}}} p_0(x) w^{(1)}(x)dx, & \\ &\\ f_{2,1}= {\displaystyle \int _{\Delta ^{(2)}}} p_0(x) w^{(2)}(x)dx, & f_{2,2}= {\displaystyle \int _{\Delta ^{(2)}}} p_1(x)w^{(2)}(x)dx. \end {array} \end{equation}

Hence, simultaneous Gaussian quadrature rules associated with MOPs reduces to the computation of the eigendecomposition of the Hessenberg matrix \(H_n,\) which, unfortunately, turns out to be strongly ill–conditioned [6, 8]. Furthermore, the Matlab function balance.m applied to the Hessenberg matrix \(H_n\) does not improve the condition of the eigenvalue problem [6] and, then, the Matlab function eig.m yields unreliable results. Therefore, procedures for computing simultaneous Gaussian quadrature rules are implemented with variable precision arithmetic [8].

In this talk we describe a new algorithm for computing simultaneous Gaussian quadrature rules.

The first step of the algorithm is to construct a balancing matrix that, applied to \(H_n, \) drastically reduces the condition of the Hessenberg eigenvalue problem, thereby allowing to compute the associated simultaneous Gaussian quadrature rule in floating point arithmetic in a reliable way (see [6], https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cam.2024.116109). Specifically, this step consists of determining a diagonal matrix \(\Delta _n= {\tt diag}(\delta _1,\ldots ,\delta _n)\), balancing \(H_n,\) i.e., \(\hat {H}_n= \Delta _n^{-1} H_n\Delta _n, \) such that \({\tt triu}(\hat {H}_n,-1) \) is symmetric, where triu\((A, k)\) denotes the elements on and above the \(k\)th diagonal of \(A.\)

The second step of the algorithm consists of computing the eigenvalue decomposition of \(\hat {H}_n\). This can be done by applying the \(QR\) algorithm to \(\hat {H}_n\), requiring \(\mathcal {O} (n^3) \) floating point operations and \(\mathcal {O} (n^2) \) memory.

In order to reduce complexity and memory, for the second step we propose an alternative procedure based on a variant of the Aberth–Ehrlich method to compute the eigenvalues and associated left and right eigenvectors, requiring only \({\mathcal O}(n)\) memory and \({\mathcal O}(n^2)\) floating point operations [6].

As a numerical example, we consider the computation, carried out in Matlab R2022a, with machine precision \(\varepsilon \approx 2.22\times 10^{-16}\), of

\[\int _{\Delta ^{(i)}}f(x) w_i^{(1)}(x)dx,\quad \int _{\Delta ^{(i)}}f(x) w_i^{(2)}(x)dx, \]

where the integrand function is \(f(x)= x e^{-x}, \) and \(w_i^{(1)}, w_i^{(2)},\; i=1,\ldots , 9, \) are the weight functions associated with the MOPs described in [6], whose corresponding simultaneous Gaussian quadrature rules are denoted by \({\bf MOP}_i, \; i=1,\ldots , 9, \) in Tables 1, 2, and 3.
